BUDGETING

Don't blow it

INTRODUCTION

Budgeting may seem scary at first, even boring, but in reality, it’s a simple and effective way to control your finances. Whether you get a monthly allowance or work at your local Kumon, everyone should at least develop a simple savings plan to track their expenses. If you don’t have a steady income right now, it’s never too early to learn the basics of budgeting for the future.

SMART BUDGETING

You might picture a detailed record of every single penny you spend when you think of budgeting, but this method has actually been proven ineffective. Even the most skilled financial planners don’t track each one of their purchases. If we split up the amount of money we get within a year and allocate an equal amount to each month, shopping for Christmas gifts would be a disaster and you might not be able to cover that unexpected car repair.

Instead, you should keep track of your main expenses and use what you have leftover for spending. If you have a credit or debit card, it’s easy since most banks have an integrated system to track your expenses. Find out how much of your income goes to your necessary expenses like rent, gas, food, and savings, subtract that from your total income, and you get the amount you can spend.

SAVING

A big part of a financial plan is figuring out how much to save each month. You may be inclined to spend more and save less but that’ll cost you in the long run. It’s crucial to save enough money every month to have an emergency fund. An emergency fund is a fund set aside that covers at least three months’ worth of expenses. Having an emergency fund is super handy; it allows you to switch jobs comfortably, have a peace of mind, and if worse comes to worst, unemployment.

The teenager and young adult mind are quick to think that they’re invincible but unexpected events will happen! Nobody thought the coronavirus would be a huge deal but here we are. It’s important to prepare for events like these. Your emergency fund can even benefit you since lives in a savings account that gains interest.

LIVING BELOW YOUR MEANS

Having a set amount of money to spend each month may seem unappealing, but that’s only because most of us are used to living lavishly. Budgeting is a logical way to structure your spending, but changing your mindset on living standards will make you happier with spending less. Buying the latest phone or the biggest house may give you some temporary joy but if you’re living paycheck to paycheck, you’ll be stressed in the long term. Living below your means is easier said than done, but if you change your habits slowly, you’ll get there. Start with little things, like what groceries you buy, and eventually apply this thinking to your everyday life.

Living below your means will not only make your bank account happier, but it’ll also help you develop important life skills like self-control. One of the most challenging obstacles you’ll have to face is FOMO (Fear Of Missing Out). You’ll see your friends splurging on luxury items, but if you follow in their path, you might be struggling within years.
